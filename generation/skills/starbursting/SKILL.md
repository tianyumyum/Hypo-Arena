## Analytical Framework: Starbursting

Apply the Starbursting method to generate hypotheses from the case context.
This method ensures comprehensive coverage by systematically examining the
case through six distinct analytical dimensions.

### Core Concept

Standard analysis often focuses on the most obvious dimension (typically "what
happened" or "how it happened"). Starbursting forces examination across all
six dimensions to prevent analytical blind spots and reveal interactions between
factors that single-dimensional analysis would miss.

The six dimensions are:
- **What** (何事): Which specific component, system, or process failed?
- **When** (何时): Why did the failure occur at this specific time?
- **Where** (何地): How did location/environment contribute?
- **Who** (何人): Whose actions/decisions were involved?
- **How** (如何): What was the failure mechanism?
- **Why** (为何): What are the deeper root causes?

### Procedure

1. **Dimensional exploration**: For each of the six dimensions, generate 1-3
   specific hypotheses that focus on that aspect:
   - What: Component/system failures, process deviations
   - When: Timing factors, sequence issues, temporal vulnerabilities
   - Where: Environmental conditions, spatial constraints, location-specific factors
   - Who: Human actions, decisions, training gaps, communication failures
   - How: Technical mechanisms, physical processes, failure modes
   - Why: Root causes, systemic factors, organizational pressures

2. **Cross-dimensional analysis**: Look for connections between dimensions:
   - Which hypotheses from different dimensions complement each other?
   - Are there patterns that emerge when combining certain dimensions?
   - For example: "Who" + "When" might reveal fatigue-related decision errors
   - "Where" + "How" might reveal environmental stress on specific mechanisms

3. **Synthesize integrated hypotheses**: Combine the strongest cross-dimensional
   insights into unified explanations that account for multiple factors
   simultaneously.

### Quality Criteria

- Each dimension must produce genuinely distinct hypotheses, not restatements
  of the same idea from different angles.
- Cross-dimensional synthesis must identify real interactions, not just
  list hypotheses from different dimensions side-by-side.
- Verification must specify which dimensions support the hypothesis and
  how they reinforce each other.
- The "Why" dimension should probe beyond immediate causes to underlying
  systemic or organizational factors.
