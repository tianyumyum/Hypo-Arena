## Analytical Framework: Morphological Analysis

Apply morphological analysis to generate hypotheses from the source context.
This method ensures comprehensive coverage of the hypothesis space by
systematically combining all possible values across key problem dimensions.

### Core Concept

Complex problems can be decomposed into independent dimensions. Each dimension
has multiple possible values. Morphological analysis forces examination of ALL
combinations across dimensions — including non-obvious combinations that
standard linear thinking would never consider.

### Procedure

1. **Decompose the problem into 3-5 key dimensions**: Identify the independent
   axes that define the hypothesis space. Typical dimensions include:
   - **Mechanism type**: What kind of process or cause? (structural, behavioral,
     procedural, environmental, design-level...)
   - **Trigger or catalyst**: What initiated the chain of events or pattern?
     (routine condition, abnormal state, external input, temporal shift...)
   - **Contributing condition**: What enabled the outcome to develop?
     (design weakness, degraded capacity, inadequate oversight, time pressure...)
   - **System boundary**: Where in the system or structure did the issue
     originate? (component level, subsystem, interface, organizational...)

2. **Enumerate values for each dimension**: For each dimension, list 3-5
   concrete possible values drawn from the evidence. Be specific — use
   actual entities, observations, and conditions mentioned in the context.

3. **Build the morphological space**: Construct a matrix with dimensions as
   rows and possible values as columns. The total number of combinations =
   product of values across all dimensions.

4. **Screen combinations**:
   - **Eliminate impossible combinations**: Remove combinations that are
     physically impossible or logically contradictory based on the evidence.
   - **Identify high-plausibility combinations**: Flag combinations that are
     well-supported by multiple pieces of evidence.
   - **Identify surprising combinations**: Flag non-obvious combinations that
     are not contradicted by evidence — these are the hidden hypotheses that
     standard analysis would miss.

5. **Develop top hypotheses**: For the 3-5 most promising combinations,
   develop each into a full hypothesis with an explanatory narrative showing
   how that specific combination of factors leads to the observed outcome.

### Output Mapping

Translate the methodology's analytical output into the Hypothesis–Evidence
pairs your domain instructions specify; cardinality, category labels
(where applicable), and Evidence-anchoring rules are defined there. Use
the methodology's outputs as analytical inputs, not as output structure
— do not surface internal scaffolding (matrices, scoring tables,
step-by-step procedure, dimension lists) in the final output.

### Quality Criteria

- At least 3 dimensions must be explicitly identified and populated.
- The screening step must explicitly eliminate impossible combinations
  with reasoning, not just select favorites.
- At least one "surprising" combination must be considered, even if
  ultimately deprioritized.
- Verification must trace each hypothesis back to its specific
  dimensional combination.
