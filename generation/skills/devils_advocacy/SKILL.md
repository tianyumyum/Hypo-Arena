## Analytical Framework: Devil's Advocacy

Apply Devil's Advocacy to generate hypotheses from the source context.
This method ensures that the most obvious explanation has been subjected
to the strongest possible challenge before being accepted.

### Core Concept

Devil's Advocacy differs from casual self-critique. A half-hearted challenge
("well, maybe it could be something else...") actually reinforces the
original conclusion by creating a false sense of thoroughness. Effective
Devil's Advocacy requires genuinely adopting the opposing position and
building the STRONGEST case you can — as if your career depended on proving
the leading hypothesis wrong.

### Procedure

1. **State the leading hypothesis clearly**: Read the context and identify
   the most obvious, most intuitive explanation. Write it out explicitly
   with its supporting evidence and reasoning chain.

2. **Commit to the opposing position**: Adopt the stance: "I am convinced
   this explanation is wrong. My task is to prove it." This is not a
   thought experiment — reason as if you genuinely believe it.

3. **Attack the evidence**:
   - **Reliability**: Which pieces of evidence are based on assumptions,
     estimates, or secondhand reports rather than direct observation?
     Could any measurements be inaccurate or misinterpreted?
   - **Completeness**: What evidence is MISSING that should be present if
     the leading hypothesis were true? Absence of expected evidence is
     itself diagnostic.
   - **Alternative interpretation**: For each key piece of evidence, propose
     at least one alternative interpretation that is consistent with a
     different hypothesis.

4. **Attack the reasoning**:
   - **Assumptions**: What implicit assumptions connect the evidence to
     the conclusion? Which are unverified?
   - **Logical gaps**: Where does the causal chain have unsupported jumps?
     Where is correlation being treated as causation?
   - **Anchoring**: Is the leading hypothesis dominant because it was
     considered first, or because it's genuinely strongest?

5. **Construct the alternative**: Build the strongest alternative hypothesis
   from the weaknesses identified. This is not a random alternative — it
   must specifically address the vulnerabilities found in steps 3-4 and
   explain the evidence that the leading hypothesis handles poorly.

6. **Assess the result**: Having built the strongest opposing case:
   - If the alternative is genuinely compelling: present it as the primary
     hypothesis, with the original as a considered alternative.
   - If the leading hypothesis survives the challenge: present it refined
     and strengthened by having addressed the vulnerabilities. Note which
     attacks it survived and which required refinement.
   - If both are plausible: present both with an honest assessment of which
     evidence would distinguish between them.

### Quality Criteria

- The leading hypothesis must be stated explicitly before the challenge
  begins. The reader must see what is being challenged.
- The attacks must be substantive — targeting specific evidence and logic,
  not offering vague doubts.
- The alternative hypothesis must be genuinely built from identified
  weaknesses, not manufactured from thin air.
- The final assessment must honestly report whether the challenge
  succeeded or failed, and what was learned either way.
- Avoid strawman attacks — challenge the strongest version of the leading
  hypothesis, not a weakened caricature.
